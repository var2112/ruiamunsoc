Our Theme '22

Preservation of Democracy, Equality & Civil Liberty

The fundamental tenets of all democracies and societies under the rule of law are human rights. The fundamental significance of human rights has been widely accepted since the end of World War II. It is even more crucial today, in light of the numerous conflicts, humanitarian crises, and serious transgressions of international law, that policy responses be firmly rooted in human rights and that states uphold the legally binding obligations they entered into when ratifying international human rights treaties. International human rights legislation offers a crucial foundation and direction for responsible and sustainable policy-making in a variety of areas, including the battle against violent extremism, the effort to eradicate poverty, and our method of managing migration.

Parliamentarians are closely connected to the issues of the populace. Parliaments may advance the fundamental principles of respect, communication, and compromise at a time when our society is becoming more polarised. Discriminatory and xenophobic discourse has no place in society since it damages societies and causes communities to become more polarised. Parliaments that create effective laws, accurately reflect the variety of their societies, and hold their administrations accountable have the capacity to significantly influence the direction of their nations' futures. It is not impossible to recognise that members of particular groups may require more protection while adhering to the ideals of equality, universality, and non-discrimination. Specific human rights instruments and processes have been created in order to defend the rights of women as well as certain groups, including detainees, migrant workers, stateless people, refugees, displaced people, minorities, indigenous peoples, children, and people with disabilities.

Throughout the years, we have fought for civil liberties, and we are aware that occasionally the light gets a little dimmer, which puts democracy in jeopardy. Now, civil liberties have been lost in many other nations, partly as a result of the unrest in the rest of the world. Of course, maintaining freedom of the press, freedom of expression, and freedom of assembly while at war is difficult. They instantly vanish. Therefore, they have left numerous nations where they were formerly secure. In other nations, not only did freedom of the press, freedom of assembly, and freedom of speech disappear before the start of the conflict, but also freedom of religion. We are aware of the serious obligation we bear as citizens of this nation. We live in harmony. We must protect democratic liberties because we have no justification for the anxieties that dominate so many other people throughout the world.
